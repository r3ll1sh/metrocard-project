MetroCard Kiosk

Storyboarding Project


I chose to focus on the kiosks in the New York City subway. The NYC Subway is used by millions of riders, and is one of the largest systems in the world. Riders use MetroCards to pay, which they buy at kiosks in most stations.


The kiosks, officially known as MetroCard Vending Machines (MVMs), allows for the purchase of new MetroCards or the refill of existing cards, accepting cash or credit card. They were created in 1999 by the design firm Antenna.

Questions

To examine the design of the kiosks, I first developed a set of questions to ask kiosk users:

  • When do you use the kiosks? Do you ever plan to use them in advance?
    • Most users report using the kiosks only when they realize they need to, and don't plan to use them in advance.
  • What are you usually trying to do at a kiosk?
    • Users unanimously reported trying to refill a MetroCard they already own.
  • How familiar are you with all the screens? How quickly are you able to find what you're looking for?
    • Most users said that they sometimes have trouble navigating to the screen they want, and are unfamiliar with the structure of the options available.
  • Does the layout of the payment panels on the machine make sense to you?
    • Most users found the panels to be logical, but a few still experienced difficulty. For example, some users confused the MetroCard slot with the credit card slot when paying.

Observations

I also observed several users interacting with the kiosk, and recorded the following observations:

  • Most riders attempt to make their selections as quickly as possible. Many were in a hurry to catch a train.
  • Users spend almost all of their time on the screen.
  • Users sometimes have to go back because they made the wrong selection.
  • Some riders are children, elderly, or inebriated. These users had a difficult time understanding the options and selecting correctly.

Conclusion

Of course, there are a variety of other design considerations for these kiosks. The kiosks were made intentionally bulky to prevent vandalism and make cleaning easier. Additionally, they must be accessible to riders with disabilities. However, I believe that New Yorkers deserve a better kiosk, using new technology and a people-centered design.
